Cauchy-Schwarz inequality

Let \[ p(t) = \|t \vec{y} - \vec{x}\|^2 \]

Since there's an absolute value, it must be greater than or equal to 0: \[ p(t) = \|t \vec{y} - \vec{x}\|^2 \geq 0 \]

Expanding p(t) using the dot product: \[ p(t) = (t \vec{y} - \vec{x}) \cdot (t \vec{y} - \vec{x}) \geq 0 \] \[ p(t) = t^2 (\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y}) - 2t (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}) + (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x}) \geq 0 \]

Let's substitute terms for simplicity: \[ p(t) = t^2\underbrace{(\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y})}_{\text{a}} + t\underbrace{(-2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y})}_{\text{b}} + \underbrace{(\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x})}_{\text{c}} \geq 0 \]

So, \[ p(t) = at^2 + bt + c \geq 0 \]

The minimum value of this quadratic function occurs at \(t = -\frac{b}{2a}\).

Substitute \(t = -\frac{b}{2a}\): \[ p\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) = a \left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 + b \left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) + c \geq 0 \] \[ p\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) = a \frac{b^2}{4a^2} + b \left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) + c \geq 0 \] \[ p\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) = \frac{b^2}{4a} - \frac{b^2}{2a} + c \geq 0 \]

Now, simplify without p(t): \[ \frac{b^2}{4a} - \frac{b^2}{2a} + c \geq 0 \]

Multiply by 4a: \[ b^2 - 2b^2 + 4ac \geq 0 \] \[ -b^2 + 4ac \geq 0 \] \[ 4ac \geq b^2 \]

De-substitute back: \[ p(t) = t^2\underbrace{(\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y})}_{\text{a}} + t\underbrace{(-2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y})}_{\text{b}} + \underbrace{(\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x})}_{\text{c}} \geq 0 \]

Then: \[ 4 (\vec{y} \cdot \vec{y})(\vec{x} \cdot \vec{x}) \geq (-2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y})^2 \]

Using the identity \(\vec{v} \cdot \vec{v} = \|\vec{v}\|^2\): \[ 4 \|\vec{y}\|^2 \|\vec{x}\|^2 \geq (-2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y})^2 \]

Applying \((f(x))^2 = (|f(x)|)^2 (where f(x) \in \mathbb{R})\): \[ 4 \|\vec{y}\|^2 \|\vec{x}\|^2 \geq (|-2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}|)^2 \]

Since both sides are non-negative, we can take the square root: \[ 2 \|\vec{y}\| \|\vec{x}\| \geq | -2 \vec{x} \cdot \vec{y} | \]

Simplify to: \[ 2 \|\vec{y}\| \|\vec{x}\| \geq 2 |\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}| \]

Thus: \[ \|\vec{y}\| \|\vec{x}\| \geq |\vec{x} \cdot \vec{y}| \]

This completes the proof of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

Independence and Uncorrelation: Key Differences

Independence and uncorrelation are two fundamental concepts in probability and statistics that often get confused but are conceptually distinct.

  1. Independence: Two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are considered independent if the occurrence of one provides no information about the occurrence of the other. Mathematically, \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent if their joint probability distribution is the product of their marginal distributions, i.e., \(P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \cdot P(Y)\) . In terms of expected values, \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent if \[ \mathbb{E}[f(X) g(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[f(X)] \cdot \mathbb{E}[g(Y)] \] for any functions \(f\) and \(g\). Independence implies a much stronger relationship, as it not only suggests that the variables don't move together but also that one variable doesn't influence or predict the distribution of the other in any way.
  2. Uncorrelation: Uncorrelation, on the other hand, is a weaker condition. Two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are uncorrelated if their covariance \[ \operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])] \] is zero. This means that their linear relationship is absent, i.e., there is no linear association between \(X\) and \(Y\). However, uncorrelation does not rule out all forms of dependence. For instance, \(X\) and \(Y\) could still have a nonlinear dependency, meaning that knowing one variable could still provide information about the other, even if it does not affect the mean behavior of the other in a linear way.

Conceptual Differences

  • Scope of Relationship: Independence addresses a complete absence of dependency, implying that all aspects of one variable are uninfluenced by the other. Uncorrelation, however, only addresses linear dependence; two uncorrelated variables might still have higher-order dependencies.
  • Implications: If two variables are independent, they are also uncorrelated (since no relationship, including a linear one, exists). However, the converse does not hold. Variables can be uncorrelated but still exhibit a non-linear relationship. A classic example is the pair \(X\) and \(Y = X^2\), where \(X\) and \(Y\) are uncorrelated (since \(\mathbb{E}[X \cdot Y] = 0)\)but clearly not independent, as \(Y\) is deterministically related to \(X\).

Measures of Independence and Uncorrelation

  • Covariance and Correlation: Uncorrelation is commonly measured through the covariance or, more practically, the correlation coefficient. The correlation coefficient \(\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}\) normalizes covariance, making it easier to assess the strength and direction of a linear relationship between \(X\) and \(Y\). A zero correlation implies uncorrelation but not necessarily independence.
  • Mutual Information: Mutual information is often used to measure independence. Unlike correlation, mutual information can capture all forms of dependenceâ€”linear and non-linear. If the mutual information between two variables is zero, they are truly independent. Mutual information is especially valuable when exploring relationships that go beyond simple linear dependencies.

While uncorrelation and independence are related concepts, they differ in scope and implications. Uncorrelation is limited to the absence of linear association, while independence implies a broader, all-encompassing lack of dependency. Covariance and correlation provide measures of uncorrelation, whereas more comprehensive measures like mutual information are required to gauge independence accurately.
